# Scale and Proportion in Interior Design

Scale governs how furniture relates to the room. Proportion governs how pieces relate to one another. Both must be balanced for a space to feel composed.

## Visual Weight

Visual weight is how “heavy” an object appears, independent of physical mass.

- Dark, bulky, low-clearance pieces read heavier.
- Light, leggy, open-frame pieces read lighter.

Balance heavy and light objects across the room to avoid one-sided visual pull.

## Ceiling Height Relationships

- Low ceilings: prefer lower-profile furniture and vertical visual lines from drapery.
- High ceilings: include taller elements (bookcases, art stacks, floor lamps) to prevent a “floating lower third.”

## Height Mixing

Use a high-mid-low rhythm in each vignette:

- High: floor lamp, tall plant, artwork.
- Mid: sofa back, console, chair.
- Low: coffee table, ottoman, low bench.

Uniform height everywhere makes rooms feel flat.

## Oversized vs Undersized Errors

- Oversized sofa in small room kills circulation.
- Tiny coffee table in front of large sectional looks disconnected.
- Slim rug under large furniture makes everything feel unstable.

## Rule of Three in Groupings

Object groupings of three with varied heights and forms often read most naturally.

Example: tall vase + medium candle + low bowl.

## Balancing Large and Small Pieces

- Pair one large anchor with multiple scaled secondary items.
- Repeat shape language (rounded, linear, tapered) to keep cohesion.
- Leave negative space around larger pieces to preserve breathing room.
