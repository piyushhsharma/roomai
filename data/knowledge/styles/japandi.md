# Japandi Interior Design Guide

Japandi blends Japanese wabi-sabi restraint with Scandinavian practicality. The result is calm, tactile, and intentional: fewer objects, stronger craftsmanship, and generous negative space.

## Fusion Logic

- Japanese influence: asymmetry, imperfection, low visual noise, natural patina.
- Scandinavian influence: usability, comfort, clean joinery, everyday functionality.
- Shared values: simplicity, nature, craftsmanship, quality over quantity.

## Color Direction

Use muted earthy tones:

- Foundations: warm off-white, mushroom, clay, stone, pale taupe.
- Supporting tones: muted olive, smoke blue, charcoal, umber.
- Contrast: black or very dark brown in thin lines (frames, legs, hardware).

Avoid high-saturation accent colors and overly bright whites.

## Negative Space as a Design Tool

Empty space is active composition, not unused area.

- Leave breathing room between major furniture pieces.
- Avoid filling every wall with art.
- Limit open-shelf objects to curated groups.
- Use one statement object per zone instead of many small accents.

## Low Furniture Philosophy

- Keep seat heights and storage profiles visually low.
- Use low credenzas and platform beds where possible.
- Balance low furniture with vertical elements (slim floor lamp, branch arrangement, tall plant) to avoid a “flattened” room.

## Texture and Craft

Key textures:

- Raw or lightly treated wood.
- Linen, wool, cotton, paper-like shades.
- Handmade ceramic, stone, and plaster textures.

Prefer visible hand-crafted irregularities over machine-perfect gloss.

## Decluttering Rules

- Keep horizontal surfaces at least 70% clear.
- Store frequently used items in closed storage, not visual display.
- Edit decorative objects monthly; remove anything with no emotional or functional value.

## Plant Integration

Plants should feel architectural, not decorative clutter:

- Use fewer, sculptural species (olive, ficus, dracaena, bonsai-like forms).
- Place in matte ceramic or stone planters.
- Keep plant heights varied for composition.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating Japandi as empty minimalism without warmth.
- Mixing too many wood tones without hierarchy.
- Adding boho-level decor density.
- Using cool industrial lighting instead of warm layered light.
