# Minimalist Interior Design Guide

Minimalism is disciplined subtraction. It emphasizes proportion, material quality, and calm visual hierarchy rather than emptiness for its own sake.

## Reduction Principle

- Remove non-essential objects and furniture.
- Keep only what is useful, beautiful, or both.
- Ensure each item has clear placement and purpose.

## Palette and Material

- Predominantly monochromatic schemes: white, beige, stone, grey, black.
- Use tonal variation to prevent flatness.
- Introduce depth through texture: plaster, linen, matte wood, wool.

## Spatial Clarity

- Maintain generous breathing room between objects.
- Avoid wall-to-wall furniture distribution.
- Let negative space define luxury and calm.

## Hidden Storage

Minimalist rooms fail without storage planning.

- Closed cabinetry over open display.
- Integrated media and cable concealment.
- Entry drop zones hidden from primary sight lines.

## Furniture as Sculpture

With fewer objects, each piece matters more:

- Favor strong silhouettes and craftsmanship.
- Use fewer but higher-quality pieces.
- Maintain consistent material language.

## Avoiding Sterility

Minimal does not mean clinical:

- Add warm undertones in textiles and woods.
- Layer warm lighting and dimmable sources.
- Include one organic element (plant, branch, stone vessel).
- Keep tactile contrast between smooth and soft surfaces.

## Common Mistakes

- Purchasing many cheap “minimal-looking” pieces.
- Over-reliance on cool white light.
- Bare rooms without textural richness.
- Removing personality entirely; restraint should still feel human.
