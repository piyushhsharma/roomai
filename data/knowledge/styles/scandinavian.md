# Scandinavian Interior Design Guide

Scandinavian design is a climate-informed design language developed in Nordic countries where winters are long and daylight is limited. The core objective is to create bright, livable, emotionally warm interiors that remain practical for daily life.

## Core Principles

- Function before decoration: every piece should serve a clear purpose.
- Simplicity with comfort: minimal forms, but never cold or hostile.
- Light amplification: surfaces, textiles, and lighting choices should increase perceived brightness.
- Natural honesty: wood grain, linen texture, and hand-finished surfaces are preferred over glossy synthetic finishes.

## Neutral Palette Rules

Use a layered neutral base instead of flat white-only rooms.

- Base whites: soft whites with warm undertones, not stark blue whites.
- Mid neutrals: light greys, greige, oatmeal, and sand.
- Deeper accents: charcoal, forest green, muted navy, or black used sparingly.
- Recommended split: 60% light neutral walls, 30% wood/textile mid tones, 10% contrast accents.

Avoid combining cool white walls with cool grey floors in low-light rooms; this often reads clinical.

## Materials and Finishes

Primary materials:

- Woods: pine, ash, birch, white oak.
- Textiles: wool bouclé, linen, cotton, sheepskin.
- Stone/ceramic: matte, lightly textured finishes.

Prioritize matte or low-sheen finishes. High gloss surfaces reflect light harshly and undermine the soft atmosphere.

## Functional Minimalism

Scandinavian minimalism is not emptiness. It is disciplined editing:

- Keep only daily-use furniture visible.
- Use integrated or concealed storage to reduce visual noise.
- Maintain clear circulation paths and avoid overfilling room corners.
- Use fewer, better items with good ergonomics and durability.

## Hygge in Practice

Hygge is emotional comfort translated into spatial decisions:

- Layered textiles on seating.
- Warm pools of light at human height (table lamps, floor lamps).
- Rounded forms and natural tactility.
- Micro-zones for reading, tea, or conversation.

## Lighting Strategy for Dark Climates

Because daylight can be limited, rely on layered artificial lighting:

- Ambient: diffuse ceiling fixture.
- Task: directional lamp at work or reading areas.
- Accent: wall wash, shelf light, or small table lamps.

Use warm white bulbs in living and bedroom spaces (typically around 2700K) and keep dimmers where possible.

## Typical Furniture Forms

- Sofas with clean silhouettes and exposed wood or slim legs.
- Dining chairs with curved backs and light visual mass.
- Coffee tables with rounded corners or soft edges.
- Storage units with plain fronts and minimal hardware.

Prefer furniture with visible floor clearance to preserve an airy feel.

## What to Avoid

- Overly ornate trims, heavy drapery, and dark bulky furniture.
- Too many decorative objects on open surfaces.
- Cold monochrome schemes without wood and textile warmth.
- Single overhead light as the only source at night.
